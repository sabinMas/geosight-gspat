# GeoSight Geospatial Concepts

## FEMA flood zones in GeoSight
GeoSight currently reads FEMA NFHL flood-zone codes for US points and turns them into a plain-language label. `Zone X` is treated as minimal mapped flood hazard and scores highest in the deterministic flood factor. `Zone A` and `Zone AE` are Special Flood Hazard Areas and receive the strongest penalty because FEMA marks them as high-risk floodplain designations. `Zone AO` and `Zone AH` are also Special Flood Hazard Area categories, typically tied to shallow flooding or ponding. `Zone V` and `Zone VE` are coastal high-hazard categories, also Special Flood Hazard Areas. `Zone B`, `Zone C`, and `Zone D` are lower-confidence or moderate-risk contexts and score in the middle rather than at the top. GeoSight does not interpret a missing FEMA result as safe; it labels it unavailable or unsupported.

## Air quality and AQI thresholds
GeoSight uses two related air-quality ideas. When OpenAQ station data is present, it classifies PM2.5 or PM10 into the familiar public-health bands: `Good`, `Moderate`, `Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups`, `Unhealthy`, `Very Unhealthy`, and `Hazardous`. When OpenAQ is missing but Open-Meteo returns a US AQI value, GeoSight maps that number into the same broad health buckets with breakpoints at `50`, `100`, `150`, and `200`. In the scoring layer, good air quality scores highest, moderate conditions are usable but not ideal, and higher AQI bands create stronger penalties for hiking and residential interpretation.

## Seismic classifications in GeoSight today
GeoSight does not yet assign a formal building-code seismic zone. Instead, it summarizes recent earthquake activity from the USGS event feed: event count within `250 km` over the last `30 days`, strongest magnitude, and nearest event distance. In practical GeoSight interpretation, a low-activity context means no recent events or only a few small events, a moderate context means recurring smaller events, and an elevated context means either many events or a strongest recent event around magnitude `4.5` or higher. This is a recent-event screen, not a probabilistic hazard map, so it should be read as "recent activity nearby" rather than "formal seismic zoning."

## Elevation and terrain terminology
`Elevation` in GeoSight is a point sample in meters, usually from USGS EPQS in the US or OpenTopoData SRTM elsewhere. `Elevation profile` means a sampled transect across the active region, not a full slope raster. `Terrain buildability` is the residential and commercial interpretation that rewards lower, flatter terrain. `Terrain variety` is the hiking interpretation that rewards moderate relief and scenic variation rather than flatness. `Cooling terrain` is the data-center interpretation that rewards relatively lower, flatter sites because they tend to be easier to build and cool. None of these terrain terms claim parcel-grade engineering certainty; they are first-pass spatial signals.

## Provenance in the GeoSight context
In GeoSight, provenance means the user can tell where a conclusion came from, how fresh it is, whether it is direct or derived, and what region it truly covers. A provenance-aware card or answer should show the provider, source status (`live`, `derived`, `limited`, `unavailable`, or `demo`), freshness window, coverage statement, and a confidence note. Provenance is not just citation text. It is the combination of source metadata, fallback behavior, evidence kind, and honest disclosure when coverage is US-only, Washington-only, or only approximate.
